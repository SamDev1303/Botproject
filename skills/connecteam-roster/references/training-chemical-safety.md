## 1. The Colour-Coded Cleaning System

To prevent cross-contamination (spreading bacteria from dirty areas to clean areas), we strictly adhere to the standard industry colour-coding system.

### üî¥ RED ‚Äî High Risk / Sanitary Areas
- **Use for:** Toilets, urinals, and bathroom floors immediately surrounding toilets
- **Rule:** Never use red cloths or mops outside of the toilet area

### üü° YELLOW ‚Äî Washroom Surfaces (Low Risk)
- **Use for:** Sinks, mirrors, showers, tiled walls, vanity bench tops, and changing room benches
- **Rule:** Use in bathrooms, but strictly for surfaces other than the toilet itself

### üü¢ GREEN ‚Äî Food Preparation & Service Areas
- **Use for:** Kitchen benches, stove tops, microwaves, pantry shelves, dining tables, and bar areas
- **Rule:** Crucial for preventing food-borne illness. Keep these cloths pristine

### üîµ BLUE ‚Äî General Low-Risk Areas
- **Use for:** Office desks, window sills, glass cleaning, dusting, skirtings, and general waste bins
- **Rule:** Used for general areas not requiring high-level disinfection

### Cloth Management
- Wash colour-coded cloths separately
- Replace microfibre cloths every 3‚Äì6 months or when worn
- Never use a cloth from a higher-risk area in a lower-risk area
- Each cleaner should carry a set of all 4 colours

| Chemical Type | Typical Colour | Usage |

## 3. Dilution Ratios & Mixing Rules

Incorrect dilution is a major safety hazard and cost issue.

### Why It Matters
- **Too Strong:** Damages surfaces, wastes money, causes respiratory issues
- **Too Weak:** Fails to clean or kill bacteria, wastes time

### Common Ratios (Examples Only ‚Äî Always Check Label)
| Ratio | Strength | Example Mix | Typical Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1:10** | Heavy Duty | 100ml chemical in 1L bottle | Heavy degreasing, mould treatment |
| **1:20** | Medium Duty | 50ml chemical in 1L bottle | Bathroom cleaning, disinfecting |
| **1:50** | General Cleaning | 20ml chemical in 1L bottle | Bench tops, general surfaces |
| **1:100** | Light Duty/Floors | 100ml chemical in 10L mop bucket | Floor mopping, light maintenance |

### How to Mix Safely
1. **Always fill the bucket or bottle with WATER FIRST**
2. **Add the chemical SECOND**
   - **Why?** Adding water to a concentrated chemical causes it to foam up and splash back into your eyes
3. Use measuring cups ‚Äî don't guess
4. Label all spray bottles with the chemical name and dilution ratio
5. Mix in a well-ventilated area
6. Wear gloves and eye protection when mixing

## 4. DANGEROUS COMBINATIONS ‚Äî Never Mix!

‚ö†Ô∏è **Mixing chemicals can create deadly gases. NEVER mix the following:**

### Deadly Combinations
1. **Bleach + Ammonia = Chloramine Gas** (Toxic, damages lungs/eyes)
   - ‚ö†Ô∏è Many glass cleaners and urine removers contain ammonia
2. **Bleach + Acid = Chlorine Gas** (Deadly)
   - ‚ö†Ô∏è Toilet bowl cleaners and vinegar are often acidic
3. **Bleach + Rubbing Alcohol = Chloroform** (Causes unconsciousness)
4. **Hydrogen Peroxide + Vinegar = Peracetic Acid** (Corrosive to skin, eyes, lungs)

### General Rules
- **Never mix different brands** of cleaning products, even if they look the same
- **Never mix "leftover" chemicals** from different bottles
- **Rinse surfaces thoroughly** before applying a different chemical
- **When in doubt, DON'T mix** ‚Äî ask your supervisor
- **If you accidentally mix chemicals:**
  1. Leave the area immediately
  2. Open windows/doors from a distance if safe
  3. Call 000 if you feel unwell
  4. Inform your supervisor

## 5. Safety Data Sheets (SDS)

Formerly known as MSDS (Material Safety Data Sheets).

### What Is an SDS?
A document that lists the hazards, first aid measures, ingredients, and safe handling procedures of a chemical product.

### Key Sections of an SDS
1. **Section 1:** Product identification (name, manufacturer)
2. **Section 2:** Hazard identification (danger symbols, warnings)
3. **Section 4:** First aid measures (**most important for cleaners**)
4. **Section 7:** Handling and storage requirements
5. **Section 8:** PPE requirements

### Your Responsibilities
- **Know where the SDS folder is kept** (Cleaner's van, app, or site folder)
- If a chemical splashes in an eye or is swallowed, **consult the SDS immediately**
- Call **Poisons Information Centre: 13 11 26** or **000** for emergencies
- **Do not use a chemical if the label is missing or unreadable**
- Report missing SDS to your supervisor

## 6. Safe Storage Rules

### Storage Requirements
- Store chemicals in a **cool, dry place** away from direct sunlight
- Keep lids **tightly closed** when not in use
- Store on **stable shelving** ‚Äî heavy containers on the bottom
- Keep chemicals **separated by type** (acids separate from bleach/alkaline products)
- Store away from **food, drinks, and personal items**

### Critical Rules
- ‚ùå **NEVER store cleaning chemicals in food containers** (old water bottles, juice jugs)
  - This is a leading cause of accidental poisoning in Australia
- ‚ùå **NEVER leave chemicals unattended** in client homes (especially NDIS properties)
- ‚ùå **NEVER store chemicals in direct sunlight or hot vehicles** for extended periods
- ‚úÖ **Always keep chemicals in original, labelled containers**
- ‚úÖ **Ensure child-proof caps are secured** when working in homes with children

### Vehicle Storage
- Store chemicals **upright** in a dedicated caddy/tray
- Secure the caddy so it doesn't tip during transport
- Keep an SDS folder in the vehicle
- Carry a spill kit (absorbent material, gloves, plastic bags)

## 7. Ventilation Requirements

### When Ventilation Is Required
- Using **any spray chemical** in an enclosed room
- Using **bleach, oven cleaner, or mould remover**
- Using **degreasers** in kitchens
- **Descaling** bathrooms with acid cleaners
- Any time you can **smell fumes**

### How to Ventilate
- **Open windows and doors** before starting chemical application
- Turn on **exhaust fans** (bathroom, range hood)
- If no ventilation is possible, **take regular breaks** in fresh air
- Use a **respirator/face mask** in poorly ventilated spaces

### Warning Signs ‚Äî Leave the Area If:
- You feel dizzy or lightheaded
- Your eyes start watering or burning
- You have difficulty breathing
- You experience a persistent headache
- You smell an unusually strong or chemical odour

### First Aid for Chemical Inhalation
1. Move to **fresh air immediately**
2. Loosen tight clothing
3. If breathing is difficult, call **000**
4. If conscious, sit upright to help breathing
5. Report to supervisor and complete incident report

## 8. Dwell Time ‚Äî Making Chemicals Work Properly

Many cleaners waste chemicals by spraying and wiping immediately. For effective cleaning:

| Product | Dwell Time | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Disinfectant | 5‚Äì10 minutes | Allows time to kill bacteria |
| Degreaser | 3‚Äì5 minutes | Softens baked-on grease |
| Mould remover | 10‚Äì15 minutes | Penetrates mould roots |
| Toilet bowl cleaner | 5‚Äì10 minutes | Breaks down stains |
| Descaler | 5‚Äì10 minutes | Dissolves mineral deposits |
